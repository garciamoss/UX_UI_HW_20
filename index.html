Intro

Overview

Several student groups chose from a list of federal government websites to redesign using UX/UI principles; my group selected the Department of Labor (DOL). During the five weeks to complete the assignment, we researched and identified user accessibility issues on the website. Our methodologies included usability & accessibility heuristics, UI systems & navigation, and UI & A/B Testings. Deliverables included documentation of various UX analyses during the assignment’s discovery phase, personas, a style guide ensuring consistency of various branding components, and iterations of high-fidelity clickable mobile and desktop prototypes. Due to time contraints, we limited our scope to the homepage and several key subordinate pages.

About DOL

The United States Department of Labor is one of the executive departments of the U.S. federal government. Its mission is to foster, promote, and develop the welfare of the wage earners, job seekers, and retirees of the United States; improve working conditions; advance opportunities for profitable employment; and assure work-related benefits and rights. In carrying out this mission, the Department of Labor administers and enforces more than 180 federal laws and thousands of federal regulations; they cover many workplace activities for about 10 million employers and 125 million workers. It is headed by the Secretary of Labor, who reports directly to the President and is a member of the president's Cabinet.

Discover

Heuristic Evaluations

My initial heuristic review of the homepage revealed usability challenges. The current page’s design is inelegant, having no discernible color scheme, meaningful images, or relevant content above the fold on topics within the DOL purview. COVID, an issue better suited for HealthData.gov, dominates the hero banner instead of COBRA or OSHA. A tap on any navbar item reveals a long list of sublevel pages that offer no helpful wayfinding guidance. There are FAQ and Search features, but they often lead users to intrasite link farms.

Below the Fold comprises Feature Stories, blog topics, news headlines, and Twitter feeds—helpful information elsewhere on the website but not on the homepage. However, Quick Links is a practical navigational guide for visitors to acquire specific details directly and swiftly; moving it above the fold is a sensible choice in the Site Map reorganization.

Jobs to Be Done

Using the JTBD methodology, I defined three primary jobs visitors can do at the DOL website:

  • Find the OSHA page to file a workplace violation complaint form.
  • Fill out a COBRA form to receive health insurance during unemployment while looking for a new job.
  • Find out which jobs will be in demand in five-ten years for career-planning before graduating high school or college.
I employed these activities as tasks in usability tests to gauge how quickly users complete each task and to reveal challenges they may encounter. Moreover, they shall be the criteria for testing the prototypes later in the project.

Phillip

Used the Search bar for navigation, but often encountered link farms. Had the shortest time to reach goals; sifting through the search results, however, is at best a guessing game.

Ellen-Sue

Link farms and dead-ends are the obstacles here in navigating the website. Despite the hindrances Ellen-Sue completed the tasks, but not without expressing frustration.

Jada

After a couple of false starts Jada completed the assigned tasks. She also encountered link farms that muddle user navigation. Had the longest time in task completion.

Taki

Taki chose to find out the DOL COVID policy regarding the workplace. After spending over ten minutes encountering several links farms, he abandoned his search.

Usability Tests

I assigned the tasks to three volunteers for my initial usability tests; the fourth participant (Taki) already had a topic to research on the website.
